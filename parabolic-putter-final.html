The Brief

Design a ping-pong shooter that hits a target hole 5 feet away. Minimize weight used to keep it steady. Has to be purely mechanical.

This project asked us to design and build a purely mechanical launcher that could launch ping pong balls through a target board placed 5 feet away, while fitting inside a 12"×12"×24" space (at all times), with enough storage for at least 5 balls, and minimizing counterweight. No use of motors or electronics allowed. A 2-minute window on launch day to score as many points as possible.

Why we chose the pendelum kicker mechanism

Part of the prompt was to include at least one spring somewhere on the system. After research and analysis of different mechanisms, we narrowed down our selection to either a spring-powered launcher, or a pendelum kicker. The spring powered-launcher would allow us to shoot the balls with more power, however, we were worried about the variability that the spring introduces, having each release slightly different. Another concern was that we would need to increase the amount of counterweight used to keep the system steady to account for the recoil that a spring-powered launcher has. Therefore, we decided to take what we considered a more elegant approach, a kicker pendelum mechanism where the kicker would be pulled back to a pre-determined position, let go, and would hit and launch the ball consistently to a specific location. Most teams used a spring-loaded launcher. We prioritized precision over power. That repeatability is what let us hit 60 balls through the target in 2 minutes.

The Process — Prototyping

Prototype 1 — Low-res
What are the mechanics?

Cardboard, foam, pink plastic tube. The goal wasn't beauty — it was speed. We needed to understand ball reloader alignment, kicker hinge geometry, and whether a ramp was even necessary.

Kicker needs a pivot — not a rotating shaft — to swing cleanly
Ramp eliminated — added friction and unpredictability
Ball tee geometry proved more important than expected
Prototype 2 — Critical subsystem
How far can we launch?

First acrylic + PLA prototype. Isolated the kicker, tested shaft-rotation vs. kicker-on-shaft, and finalized hardware. Copper BBs added to kicker foot for mass — MATLAB showed pendulum mass plateaus around 0.27 kg.

Kicker rotates on shaft — not with it — cleaner swing arc
Copper BBs fill kicker foot to optimize momentum
Adjustable rear standoff controls release height precisely
Prototype 3 — Elevated + shorter
How does geometry change trajectory?

MATLAB plots showed a shorter elevated kicker delivers better 7' distance. The 24" envelope constraint meant the kicker couldn't swing past vertical — we added a hard stopper, which changed our force analysis and required minimal added counterweight.

Shorter arm + higher pivot → better trajectory at 7'
Stopper added to comply with 12"×12"×24" envelope
Stopper impulse not in original FBD — required re-analysis
Prototype 4 — Final
How does everything come together?

Full system: white PLA cylindrical housing, wavy acrylic walls, funnel on top, spring-loaded ball dispenser, orange tee and kicker foot, fake grass base. 19 parts. Every one drawn, modeled, and toleranced before printing or cutting.

All subsystems aligned for reliable launch-day operation
Dispenser timing matched kicker cycle across all 60 shots

03

Analysis & The Math

Pendulum Mass Optimization

The mass transfer factor approaches its maximum quickly for a light projectile like a ping pong ball. Around 0.05 kg the returns start diminishing; by 0.27 kg extra mass is essentially negligible. We filled the kicker foot with copper BBs to hit this range without overbuilding.

Optimal range: 0.05 → 0.27 kg

Trajectory Modeling

MATLAB projectile plots across launch angles 30°–50° for pendulum length 8", release height 16", and pivot height 12". Two targets plotted: large lower hole (blue) and small upper hole (red) at 7 feet. These plots defined our physical dimensions before we touched a printer.

Target angle: ~35° – 45° at 7'

Theory said: no counterweight needed.

Our free body diagram calculated the required counterweight to prevent sliding as –0.64 kg, meaning table friction alone was sufficient. Fwall = 4.12 N. Potential energy stored: 0.20 J. We were confident.

Reality said: add some weight.

When the kicker hit the hard stopper — something our static FBD didn't account for — the impulse exceeded frictional resistance. We adapted quickly, added minimal weight, kept it at the low end of the spectrum, and it held perfectly on launch day. Theory ≠ reality. Physical testing is essential.

The Ball Dispenser

My contribution
Inspired by a candy dispenser. One ball at a time, every time.

The Parabolic Putter was a full team effort across every subsystem. The ball dispensing mechanism is the part I designed from the ground up. The inspiration came from a candy dispenser — gravity does the work, and a simple gate controls the release, one at a time.

The wavy acrylic side walls weren't structurally required — they were an intentional aesthetic choice. The machine should feel interesting, not just functional. Every part had to be compatible with the rest of the assembly: tolerances, screw patterns, and spatial fit all had to integrate cleanly across the full 19-part system.

1
Funnel on top — balls load through the PLA funnel into the cylindrical reservoir.
2
Gravity feed — balls stack inside the base tube and descend one by one.
3
Rotating plate release — one ball drops onto the acrylic dispenser plate, positioning it precisely on the tee.
4
Spring return — the plate snaps back via extension spring, ready for the next ball. The only spring in the system lives here.

Key Learnings

Prototype the critical mechanism first.

The kicker was the heart of everything. Getting it into cardboard early — even roughly — let us understand pivot behavior, ball contact geometry, and release height before committing to CAD. Speed of iteration beat depth of planning every time. We iterated on tee shapes, kicker heights, and standoff positions more times than we could count.

Models are starting points, not verdicts.

Our FBD said we didn't need a counterweight. Our machine disagreed — because the stopper created an impulsive load our static analysis didn't capture. We adapted quickly. The lesson: analytical models inform decisions; physical testing validates them. You need both, in that order.

Next Steps

The dispenser cycle worked reliably, but dead time between shots still existed. A more compact mechanism and faster reload path would push the 60-ball count higher. Next time: design the reload cycle as a primary constraint from day one, not an afterthought. I'd also love to explore the kicker geometry further — there's likely more distance left on the table with a refined foot angle.
