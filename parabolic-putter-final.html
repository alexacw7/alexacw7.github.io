The Brief

Design a machine that plays ping pong for you.

ME102's Project 3 asked us to build a fully mechanical launcher capable of hitting ping pong balls through a target board placed 5 to 7 feet away — while fitting inside a 12"×12"×24" envelope, storing at least 5 balls at a time, and using as little counterweight as possible.

No motors. No electronics. Two minutes on launch day to score as many points as possible.

Most teams reached for a spring-loaded launcher. We didn't. A spring in the launch path introduces variability — each release is slightly different. Instead, we built a pendulum kicker for consistency, and put the spring where it belongs: in the ball dispenser's return mechanism. Precision over power.

From Sketch to Machine

Four prototypes.
One clean machine.

Every version answered a question. Every question got us closer.

01
Low-res
Driving question
What are the mechanics of our components?

Cardboard, foam, and a pink plastic tube. The goal wasn't beauty — it was speed. We needed to understand ball reloader alignment, whether a kicker hinge would work, and whether a ramp was necessary at all. This scrappy model surfaced the right questions before we committed to any material.

Kicker needs a pivot — not a rotating shaft — to swing cleanly
Ball must be held precisely on the tee for consistent contact
Ramp eliminated — adds friction and unpredictability
02
Critical subsystem
Driving question
How far can we launch with a kicker?

First acrylic and PLA prototype. We isolated the kicker mechanism, testing shaft rotation vs. kicker-on-shaft, and finalizing hardware. We filled the kicker foot with copper BBs for mass — the MATLAB analysis showed pendulum mass plateaus around 0.27 kg, so we stayed in that zone. The blue 3D-printed foot and adjustable standoff let us dial in release height.

Kicker rotates on shaft, not with it — cleaner swing arc
Copper BBs added to kicker foot for momentum without bulk
Adjustable standoff in rear slots controls release height
03
Elevated + shorter
Driving question
How does the kicker geometry change the trajectory?

MATLAB trajectory plots across launch angles 30°–50° showed that a shorter, elevated kicker hitting the ball at the bottom of its swing delivered far better distance than a long arm swinging from higher up. We also confronted a key constraint: the 24" envelope meant the kicker couldn't swing freely past vertical. The solution — a hard stopper — changed our force analysis and eventually required minimal added weight.

Shorter arm + higher pivot = better trajectory toward 7' target
Stopper added to comply with 12"×12"×24" constraint
Stopper impact not captured in original FBD — required re-analysis
04
Final
Driving question
How does everything come together?

The full system: white PLA cylindrical housing, wavy acrylic side walls, funnel on top, spring-loaded dispenser, orange tee and kicker foot, fake grass base. Every part drawn, CAD'd, printed or laser-cut. 19 unique components. It launched 60 balls through the target in two minutes.

All subsystems aligned and integrated for launch day
Minimal counterweight achieved — stopper absorbed most of the impact load
Ball dispenser timing matched kicker cycle reliably across all 60 shots
The Math

Theory, testing,
and the gap between them.

We didn't just build — we modeled. MATLAB scripts plotted projectile trajectories across every relevant variable. FBDs told us what forces to expect. And then physical testing told us what we'd missed.

Pendulum Mass Optimization

Using conservation of momentum, we modeled the mass transfer factor as a function of pendulum mass. The ping pong ball is so light that the factor approaches its maximum quickly — around 0.05 kg the returns start diminishing, and by 0.27 kg, extra mass is essentially negligible. We used copper BBs to stay in the effective range without overbuilding.

Optimal pendulum mass range
0.05 kg → 0.27 kg
Trajectory Modeling

MATLAB projectile trajectory plots across launch angles 30°–50° for a pendulum length of 8", release height of 16", and pivot height of 12". Two targets marked: the large lower hole (blue, 7 in. height at 7') and the small upper hole (red, 13 in. height at 7'). The plots gave us the physical dimensions to build toward — then we validated in the lab.

Optimal launch angle range
~35° – 45° for 7' target

Theory said: no counterweight needed.

Our FBD showed that the calculated counterweight to prevent sliding was –0.64 kg — meaning friction alone between the base and table would be sufficient. Fwall = 4.12 N. Potential energy stored: 0.20 J.

Reality said: add some weight.

When the kicker swung and hit the hard stopper — something our original FBD didn't account for — the impulse exceeded frictional resistance and the system rocked. We adapted, added minimal weight, kept it at the low end of the spectrum, and it held perfectly on launch day. Theory ≠ reality. Physical testing is essential.

The Ball Dispenser

Inspired by a candy machine.

While the Parabolic Putter was a full team effort across every subsystem, the ball dispensing mechanism is what I took on and designed from the ground up. The inspiration came from a simple candy dispenser — gravity-fed, one ball at a time, elegant in its simplicity.

The wavy acrylic side walls weren't structurally required. They were an intentional aesthetic choice — to make the machine feel alive and interesting, not just functional. Every part had to be compatible with the rest of the assembly: tolerances, screw patterns, and spatial fit all had to work together.

1
Funnel on top — balls are loaded by the second operator through the PLA funnel, which feeds them into the cylindrical reservoir.
2
Gravity feed — balls stack inside the base tube and descend one by one under their own weight.
3
Rotating plate release — a single ball drops onto the rotating acrylic dispenser plate, which positions it precisely for the tee.
4
Spring return — the plate snaps back via an extension spring, ready for the next ball. The only spring in the system lives here — keeping the kicker's launch path free of variability.

Hindsight as Foresight

What we learned.

Design
Prototype the critical mechanism first.

The kicker was the heart of everything. Getting it into a physical prototype early — even in cardboard — let us understand pivot behavior, ball contact geometry, and release height in a way that no amount of sketching could. Speed of iteration beat depth of planning.

Analysis
Models are starting points, not verdicts.

Our FBD said we didn't need a counterweight. Our machine disagreed — because the stopper created an impulsive load our static model didn't capture. We adapted. The lesson: analytical models inform decisions; physical testing validates them. You need both.

Next iteration
Tighten the packaging and streamline the reload.

The dispenser cycle worked — but there was still dead time between shots that a more compact, tighter mechanism could eliminate. A more reliable and faster reload would push the 60-ball count higher. Next time: design the reload path as a first-class constraint, not an afterthought.
