The Mechanism

Converting a one-directional rotational input into linear output.

Prompt: design a system for the 2026 Winter Olympics that could lift a tired athlete onto the podium. Constraint: it has to fit inside a 12×12×12" cube, take a rotational input, and output pure vertical linear motion, lifting a 2.5 lb weight with no motors, no electronics, and no adhesives.

Me and my project partner, Daria, chose the quick-return mechanism: a pin-in-slot system where a rotating wheel drives a lever through a slotted track, converting circular motion into vertical displacement. After researching and analyzing different mechanisms, we chose this one because the asymmetry of the geometry means the platform rises slowly (controlled lift) and returns quickly, which is exactly what an athlete-assist needs.

We opted to have two of these quick-return mechanisms mirroring one another, joined by a rotating handle and the raising platform. This was to provide the stability and support necessary to lift the 2.5 lb weight.

We also took on the extra optional challenge: achieving the full cycle (platform up and back down) from rotational input in a single direction. No reversing required.

Problems & Fixes

Problem
Lever Bending Under Load

The original acrylic lever was too thin and visibly bent when lifting the 2.5lb weight, causing misalignment mid-cycle.

Fix
Thickened up lever width

The lever was thickened to better support the weight and avoid cracking. Just an example of the many small iterations made to different parts to nail down the functionality & reliability of the mechanism.

Iterations
Tolerance Iterations

Being allowed no adhesives meant that iterative testing of tolerances was required to find the right clearance. In this specific example, different sized holes are being tested to fit a bushing snuggly with no glue and account for any discrepancies caused by the laser cutter. Similar iterations had to be made for the d-shaft hole, shoulder screws hole, base-wall fit, etc.

Problem
High Friction Track

The original PLA sliding track generated too much friction against the lever pin, due to the trapezoidal shape of the track and the material properties of PLA, making the mechanism stiff and inconsistent under load.

→
Fix
New Acrylic Sliding Track

Switched to a pin & slot track using laser-cut acrylic. This dramatically reduced friction and improved alignment between both mechanisms.

Problem
Cracking Friction-Fit Base

The original acrylic base used a tight friction fit to hold the walls in place. It was slightly too tight and with acrylic being brittle, it cracking under pressure during assembly.

→
Fix
PLA Base Redesign

Redesigned in PLA with a friction fit at the bottom, built-in supports running along each wall, and screw holes through the supports and walls, to have it triple secure.

Final Remarks

The importance of Prototyping

Through this whole process, I quickly found how easy it was for things to not play out exactly as expected. For example, the original track was designed specifically with that trapezoidal-sliding geometry as I thought it would provide extra support in keeping the platform in place, it wasn't until I actually tested it that I found that it over-constrained the movement of the platform, caused misalignment issues, and therefore would cause the platform to get stuck pretty easily. This is just one of the many components we had to iterate time and time again. We had to test out different shoulder screws, different tolerances to account for laser-cutting and 3d printing discrepancies, print out so many platforms that all look almost the same from afar but have minor adjustments between each iteration. Coming into this project, I knew in theory that prototyping is essential, but I didn't really expect just how much iterations we would need to go through. All while keeping the whole project under budget and finishing under time constraints. This project shifted my perception of product realization as a process, it made me appreciate the finer details and get a better sense of understanding of just how much time and effort goes into every single deliberate choice.

Working as a team

Throughout the whole process, clear communication played a crucial role in making sure things ran smoothly. Working with a partner on a project where so many design and assembly decisions had to be made, it was very important to explain the directions we wanted to take and more importantly why, to be able to weigh out different options and if necessary, compromise. A challenge was to that Fusion 360 would not allow us to both work on the file at the same time, therefore we had to be very communicative and strategic as to who was working on what specific aspect at what time and what changes were being made. We also had to set guidelines as to how we would CAD, keeping sketches and parameters as organized and clear as possible, to ensure we could understand build off of each other's work. Similarly, because of our different availability times, we could not be in the lab at the same time all the time, therefore it was crucial that we were communicative about what changes were being done to ensure we were in agreement and to be able to continue each other's work. We often delegated the designing/making of specific parts, however, communication was still essential as all these parts needed to be compatible with one another.

Next Steps

We improved the side handles to be more ergonomic, however, the middle handle (the d-shaft) could be more ergonomic. And I'd love to see this scaled up with a motor.
