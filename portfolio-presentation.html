01 · Clock Container
ME102 · Product Realization · Winter 2025
Clock Container

A laser-cut birch plywood clock-shaped container assembled entirely through friction-fit interlocking joints. No adhesives, no fasteners. Designed to hold my daily bracelets.

Biggest takeaway: Curved interlocking joints are far more sensitive to kerf variation than straight ones. A 0.0005" change in kerf was the difference between too loose and too tight. Iterative scrap testing was essential, not optional. The cardboard mock-up also revealed a fundamental flaw in my sliding door concept before any plywood was cut, saving material and time. Physical prototypes expose constraints that sketches and CAD cannot.

02 · The Athlete Lift
ME102 · Product Realization · Winter 2026
The Athlete Lift

A quick-return mechanism: a pin-in-slot system where a rotating wheel drives a lever through a slotted track, converting circular motion into vertical displacement. Two of these mechanisms mirroring one another, joined by a rotating handle and the raising platform. We also took on the extra optional challenge: achieving the full cycle from rotational input in a single direction. No reversing required.

What I learned: I quickly found how easy it was for things to not play out exactly as expected. The original track was designed with a trapezoidal-sliding geometry because I thought it would provide extra support. It wasn't until I actually tested it that I found it over-constrained the movement and caused the platform to get stuck. This is just one of the many components we had to iterate time and time again. This project shifted my perception of product realization as a process, it made me appreciate the finer details and how much time and effort goes into every single deliberate choice.

03 · The Parabolic Putter
ME102 · Product Realization · Winter 2026
The Parabolic Putter

After research and analysis, we narrowed down to either a spring-powered launcher or a pendulum kicker. The spring would give more power, but we were worried about the variability it introduces. We took what we considered a more elegant approach: a kicker pulled back to a pre-determined position, let go, hitting and launching the ball consistently. Most teams used a spring-loaded launcher. We prioritized precision over power.

Theory vs. reality: Our FBD said we didn't need a counterweight. Our machine disagreed, because the stopper created an impulsive load our static analysis didn't capture. We adapted quickly. Analytical models inform decisions; physical testing validates them. You need both, in that order. The ball dispensing mechanism was one of my biggest focuses on the project. Inspired by a candy dispenser! I wanted it to be eye-catching and interesting, not just functional.

04 · Dog Prosthetic Running Blade
ME80 · Mechanics of Materials · Winter 2026
Dog Prosthetic Running Blade

A mass-optimized J-shaped running prosthetic for a medium-sized dog with a below-knee hind-leg amputation. J-shaped blades function as elastic energy-storage devices: they bend under load, storing strain energy, and release it during push-off. Modeled as a 5-segment frame, the load was taken as 3x body weight to represent running and jumping. I was the CAD and manufacturing lead.

What this project taught: Theory, simulation, and physical testing tell different parts of the same story. The analytical model set the dimensions. FEA caught what beam theory missed: stress concentrations, curved-beam effects, load redistribution at joints. Physical testing proved the structure works. All three are necessary, in that order. And the goal, a real blade for a real dog, is still worth building toward.
