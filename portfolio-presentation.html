01 · Clock Container
ME102 · Product Realization · Winter 2025
Clock Container

A laser-cut birch plywood container shaped like a vintage alarm clock — designed to hold my daily bracelets. Assembled entirely through friction-fit interlocking joints. No glue, no fasteners. Just wood and precision.

The hard part: Kerf tolerance on curves is incredibly sensitive — 0.0005" is the difference between a loose fit and a tight one. I had to run iterative kerf tests on scrap material to nail the right value for this specific laser cutter. My cardboard prototype also killed my original sliding-door concept early, which saved me from wasting real material.

02 · The Athlete Lift
ME102 · Product Realization · Winter 2026
The Athlete Lift

A quick-return mechanism that converts one-directional rotational input into vertical linear motion. Built for the 2026 Winter Olympics — lifts a 2.5 lb weight 6 inches. No motors, no electronics, no adhesives. We also took on the optional challenge: full up-and-down cycle from rotation in a single direction.

Biggest lesson: I didn't expect just how many iterations we'd need. The original PLA track caused way too much friction — we had to switch to laser-cut acrylic. The acrylic base cracked because the friction fit was too tight — so we redesigned in PLA with built-in supports and screws. Every single part went through multiple rounds. That's where the real learning happened.

03 · The Parabolic Putter
ME102 · Product Realization · Winter 2026
The Parabolic Putter

A fully mechanical ping pong launcher. Most teams used a spring — we didn't. We built a pendulum kicker because it's deterministic: release height and geometry fully define the trajectory. Precision over power. 60 balls through the target in 2 minutes.

Theory vs. reality: Our FBD said we didn't need a counterweight. The machine disagreed — when the kicker hit the hard stopper, the impulse exceeded what our static analysis predicted. We adapted quickly, added minimal weight, and it held on launch day. Models are starting points, not verdicts. Physical testing is essential.

04 · Dog Prosthetic Running Blade
ME80 · Mechanics of Materials · Winter 2026
Dog Prosthetic Running Blade

A mass-optimized J-shaped running prosthetic for dogs with below-knee amputation. Derived analytically with a 5-segment frame model, validated with FEA, and physically load-tested to 70 lbs on a gym press machine. I was the CAD and manufacturing lead.

Where it got interesting: The sharp-edged version matched our beam theory almost perfectly (FS 1.986 vs. target 2.0). But the filleted version — the one that actually looks like a real blade — introduced curved-beam effects that raised max stress. Theory, simulation, and physical testing all told different parts of the same story. You need all three, in that order.
