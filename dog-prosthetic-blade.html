01

Intro & Why

Every year, thousands of dogs lose limbs due to trauma, cancer, or congenital defects. While amputation saves a dog's life, existing prosthetic options are often limited — wheeled carts that restore minimal mobility, or rigid pegs that allow standing but fail to replicate the dynamic mechanics of a natural gait.

We set out to design a mass-optimized J-shaped running prosthetic blade for a medium-sized dog (~30 kg) with a below-knee hind-leg amputation. J-shaped blades function as elastic energy-storage devices: they bend under load, storing strain energy, and release it during push-off — the same geometry used in human Paralympic blades, scaled for a dog.

Why mass optimization? Excess prosthetic mass at the distal end of a limb disproportionately increases rotational inertia during the swing phase, raising the energetic cost of locomotion and causing faster muscle fatigue. The goal: minimize mass while maintaining safe stress levels throughout the blade under a 300 N running load.

The blade was modeled as a 5-segment frame, fixed at the top (the socket attachment), with a ground reaction force applied upward at the toe. The load was taken as 3× body weight to represent running and jumping — where this type of prosthetic, with its high energy return, is most useful.

02

Analytical Results

Local coordinate systems were defined for each of the five segments. Equilibrium equations gave axial force N, shear V, and bending moment M(x) along each member. The key assumption: normal stress governs failure, combining axial and bending stress at |y| = h/2 from the neutral axis. FEA later confirmed this — maximum stress always occurs at the outer edges.

Setting combined stress equal to σ_allow = σ_yield / FS and solving for cross-section width b(x) gives the mass optimization: the minimum width at every point that keeps FS ≥ 2. Because M(x) is linear within each segment (no distributed loads), b(x) also varies linearly — giving smooth, implementable transitions.

For segments 1 and 5 (toe and socket regions), the analytically derived minimum width approaches zero — which would create unsafe sharp tapers. The maximum required width was used instead for these segments, prioritizing durability and balance at the attachment and ground-contact points.

N/(b·h) + M(x)·(h/2) / (b·h³/12) = σ_yield / FS

Segments 2, 3, 4 carry compressive axial loads. Euler buckling (fixed-fixed, K = 0.5) gives FS of 60 on segment 2 and 2075+ on segments 3–4. Buckling is not the governing failure mode.

Minimum b ranges from ~0 (segments 1, 5) to 51 mm (segment 3 — constant, highest moment). These values were implemented directly in CAD as the lofting profile dimensions.

03

Design

The J-blade geometry was drawn from a schematic in the Journal of Disability Research and scaled by 0.5 to suit a medium-sized dog (12.5 cm tall × 10.5 cm wide). The 5-segment simplification from the analytical model was translated directly into a parametric CAD sketch — key angles 148.7° at the top bend and 44.7° at the lower bend.

Each segment was profiled at the analytically derived widths and lofted to create a continuously varying cross-section. The profile is set at a constant height of h = 1.5 cm throughout, with only width b varying by segment. Two final designs were produced: a sharp-edged version that closely follows the straight-beam analytical model, and a filleted version for smoother, more realistic geometry.

At the junctions between segments, widths were adjusted slightly from the analytical optimum to avoid abrupt geometric discontinuities. The difference between adjacent segment widths at each junction was very small, so the larger value was used as dominant — maintaining structural continuity without significantly deviating from the optimization.

04

FEA Simulation

FEA was run in Fusion 360 Simulation on both the sharp-edged and filleted blades. Loading: top surface fixed (socket attachment), 300 N upward point load at the toe. Material: PLA defined as a custom material with yield strength 35 MPa — not available by default in Fusion.

The analytical optimization targeted a constant maximum stress of 17.5 MPa (= 35 MPa / FS 2) across the outer edges. The sharp-edged FEA closely matched: max stress 17.626 MPa, min FS 1.986. The filleted version deviated more due to curved-beam effects: max stress 19.84 MPa, min FS 1.764 — still well below yield, but a useful reminder that fillets aren't free.

The relatively uniform yellow stress contours along the outer edges of the non-filleted blade — away from junctions — visually confirmed the bending-dominance assumption and the success of the optimization approach.

Four types of discrepancy from the ideal constant-stress distribution were identified — each explained by a specific geometric or modeling choice:

Stress drops at junctions

Near sub-member connections, contours shift from yellow to green. Extra material added for smooth lofting stiffens these regions beyond what the analytical model predicts — intentional, but it means stress there is lower than the optimization target.

Sharp corners amplify stress

Localized red regions at sharp edges of the non-filleted blade reflect stress concentrations that classical beam theory (σ = My/I) cannot capture. Fillets resolve this — but introduce curved-beam effects that raise the overall maximum stress to 19.84 MPa.

Segment 3 inner-edge concentration

A localized spike at the inner edge of segment 3 slightly exceeds 17.5 MPa. Not tied to a corner or junction — likely caused by minor bending moment redistribution as the structure deflects, which the idealized piecewise-beam model doesn't capture.

Segments 1 & 5 — intentionally over-built

Very high FS and near-zero stress here (dark blue in FEA). Material was retained intentionally for stability at the ground-contact and socket-attachment points — structural function outweighs mass optimization at the extremes.

Testing method: both blades were positioned vertically between weight plates on a gym press machine. The flat plates, constrained to move only vertically by the machine, closely replicated the FEA loading conditions — a fixed top and a vertical upward force at the toe.

Loads of 20, 60, and 70 lbs were applied to both versions. Both blades held all three loads with no plastic deformation. Before-and-after photos confirmed no yielding at each step.

The design load was 300 N ≈ 67.44 lbs. Sustaining 70 lbs — above the design load — constituted successful validation. The filleted blade showed noticeably more flex under load, consistent with its lower stiffness and lower FEA factor of safety.

Potential next steps
Where this goes next
1 Carbon fiber fabrication. PLA was a reasonable prototype but carbon fiber is what running blades are actually made from — and evaluating its energy return is the most important next step.
2 Custom socket design. A limb cup interface sized to an actual dog's residual limb is the critical step before real-world use.
3 Stiffness as a constraint. Adding stiffness constraints to the mass optimization would give more control over energy return and the dog's gait experience.
4 Test it on a dog. The ultimate goal.
07

Conclusion

The approach worked.

Both designs matched analytical predictions closely. The sharp-edged blade achieved FS 1.986 and max stress 17.626 MPa — nearly exactly the target FS 2 and 17.5 MPa. Both held 70 lbs in physical testing with no plastic deformation. The mass optimization approach was validated end-to-end: hand calculations → FEA → physical test, each confirming the last.

Where it could be improved.

Mass optimization wasn't perfect at the junctions — stress dropped below the target constant value where extra material was added for smooth lofting. Future iterations should explore CAD approaches that more closely preserve the analytically derived linearly varying b(x) functions, or refine the analytical model by relaxing the rigid-junction assumption used in the piecewise beam idealization.

What this project taught.

Theory, simulation, and physical testing tell different parts of the same story. The analytical model set the dimensions. FEA caught what beam theory missed — stress concentrations, curved-beam effects, load redistribution at joints. Physical testing proved the structure works. All three are necessary, in that order. And the goal — a real blade, for a real dog — is still worth building toward.
