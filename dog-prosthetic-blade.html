01

The Brief

Design a structural component, analyze it with hand calculations, validate with FEA, and build it.

For ME80's final project, our team chose to design a J-shaped running prosthetic blade for dogs — the same geometry used in human Paralympic blades, adapted for a medium-sized dog (~30 kg). The goal: mass-optimize the cross-section dimensions of each blade segment to be as light as possible while maintaining a minimum factor of safety of 2 under a 300 N running load.

We modeled the blade as a 5-segment frame, derived internal reactions in each segment through hand calculations, set up constraint equations based on yield and deflection limits, and ran FEA in Fusion 360 to validate. We then 3D-printed two versions — one with sharp edges and one with filleted edges — and load-tested both in a gym press machine up to 70 lbs.

02

Analytical Results

5-Segment Internal Reaction Analysis

We simplified the blade into 5 segments with local coordinate axes, then applied equilibrium equations at the fixed support (top) and took sections through each segment to find internal forces and moments throughout the structure.

Key results: Segment 1 (toe, horizontal) carries pure shear and bending. Segment 2 (lower diagonal) carries a combination of axial compression and bending. Segments 3–5 carry progressively higher moments as you move toward the fixed end. The critical section is near the upper diagonal-to-horizontal transition, where bending moment is highest.

With internal reactions known at every section, we set up constraint equations — requiring the maximum normal stress (bending + axial) at each section to stay below the PLA yield strength divided by the target factor of safety — and solved for the minimum allowable cross-section width at each segment. This is the mass optimization: minimum width that keeps FS ≥ 2 everywhere.

Material: PLA

Yield strength ~35 MPa. Young's modulus ~3.5 GPa. Chosen for availability, printability, and sufficient stiffness for the scale of this prototype.

Buckling Check

Diagonal segments carry compressive axial loads. Euler buckling analysis confirmed safety factors of 60+ on segment 2 and 2075+ on segment 4 — buckling not a governing failure mode.

Optimization Output

Minimum cross-section widths derived per segment. Results compared against maximum widths from deflection limits to confirm feasibility of each dimension.

03

Design & CAD

From J-blade to 5-segment beam model

The J-shaped blade geometry was inspired by real running prosthetics. To make it analytically tractable, we simplified it into a 5-segment frame, each segment with its own local coordinate axes, allowing us to derive internal reactions (axial force N, shear V, and moment M) at cross-sections throughout the blade.

The CAD started as a sharp-edged lofted model and evolved through two major versions: sharp edges (used for the first prototype and initial FEA) and filleted edges (the final design, which reduced stress concentrations and was easier to 3D print). Cross-section widths were derived from the mass optimization and varied by segment based on the loading each one carries.

04

FEA Simulation

Setup & loading condition

FEA was run in Fusion 360 Simulation. The top attachment surface was set as a fixed constraint, matching the real-world socket attachment. A 300 N upward point load was applied at the toe of the blade, simulating peak running impact.

We ran simulations on both the sharp-edged and filleted versions to compare results. The filleted blade showed higher Von Mises stress (19.84 MPa max vs. 17.626 MPa for the sharp version) but also higher minimum factor of safety in the critical regions. Both designs remain well within PLA's yield strength of ~35 MPa.

The maximum stress concentration appeared consistently at the inner curve of the upper bend — where the bending moment is highest — aligning with the analytical prediction.

Hand calcs said: FS ≥ 2 everywhere.

The analytical model predicted all segments would meet the target factor of safety of 2 under 300 N loading, based on the optimized cross-section widths. The model assumed idealized boundary conditions and uniform cross-sections within each segment.

FEA said: min FS = 1.764 at the bend.

The FEA flagged a localized stress concentration at the inner curve of the upper bend, where the geometry transitions sharply — dropping below FS = 2. Two likely sources: the analytical model couldn't capture stress concentrations at joints, and PLA material properties show variability by print orientation. Still well below yield — the blade held 70 lbs in testing.

07

Conclusion

FEA catches what hand calcs miss.

Our analytical model assumed uniform cross-sections and idealized joints — which couldn't capture the stress concentration at the inner curve of the upper bend. FEA found a localized region where FS dropped below 2, even though the rest of the blade was fine. The two methods complement each other: hand calcs give intuition and drive design decisions, FEA validates and finds the surprises.

Print orientation matters more than you think.

PLA's material properties vary significantly with print orientation — our calculations used bulk properties, but the actual blade may have been weaker in certain directions due to layer adhesion. This is one of the likely sources of the FEA-vs-analytical discrepancy. Next time: simulate with anisotropic material properties aligned to the print direction.

Next Steps

The most important next step is material: PLA is not suitable for a real prosthetic. Carbon fiber or nylon would dramatically improve the strength-to-weight ratio and fatigue resistance. Geometrically, the socket attachment needs proper design — our idealized fixed boundary is far from a real socket. And ideally, this would be sized to an actual dog's leg measurements.
